About

I am a PhD student in the Department of History and Philosophy of Science at the University of Cambridge where I am generously funded by the Gates Cambridge Scholarship.

My research lies primarily at the intersection philosophy of artificial intelligence and empiricist philosophy of science. I am especially interested in the role of deep neural networks as scientific instruments, their role as measurement tools in cognitive neuroscience, the prospects of generating genuine scientific progress using machine learning, and how best to promote the egalitarian implementation of AI in society. Tackling these issues leads me to confront fundamental questions concerning values in science, the epistemology of scientific practice, epistemic virtues, pluralism, and pragmatism. I'm also interested in machine learning's role in our epistemic systems, especially with respect to public discourse about science and politics.

My thinking is coloured by issues of normativity more generally, including the intersections of agency and value with belief, the constitution of truth, and the indelible connection between these concepts and our social norms and practices. My approach to these topics is especially informed by historical developments in American pragmatism and feminist philosophy.

Before Cambridge I was a student at the University of Houston where I earned my BA and MA in Philosophy.
